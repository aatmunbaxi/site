## Research Interests {#research-interests}

Broadly speaking, my research interests lie in the category theory side of quantum algebra. Specifically, I am interested in braided fusion categories and modular categories that underpin the mathematics of topological quantum computing. At the risk of oversimplifying, these gadgets are categorifications of finite dimensional quasi-Hopf algebras and their behavior over \\(\mathbb{C} \\). In a very rigorous sense, this is a correct statement (keyword: Tannaka-Krein duality).

Because these gadgets are fairly new, techniques to classify them pop up occasionally with some examples accompanying the general construction. In particular, a program of Delaney, Galindo, Plavnik, Rowell, and Zhang introduced two powerful techniques: zesting (Delaney et al. 2021) and the condensed fiber product (Delaney et al. 2024).
My current interest lies in relaxations of the latter, where one drops the requirement of a local abelian symmetry (read: pointed symmetric fusion category) contained in a topological phase, in favor of an arbitrary abelian phase (read: arbitrary pointed braided subcategory).
This setting does not enjoy some of the fruits of the condensed fiber product, but with it comes the possibility of realizing new modular categories, or giving alternative realizations of existing modular categories.

Along the way, I built the [VerlindeDB](https://verlindedb.github.io/), a website made in a similar spirit as the AnyonWiki, to provide an online "database" of a some low rank modular categories coming from representations of quantum groups at roots of unity.


## Computing {#computing}

After moving away from Windows some years ago, I've inadvertently forced myself to learn some tools of the trade used in STEM-adjacent industry work, which is where I hope to end up. In particular, knowing my way around shells, version control systems, and various programming languages like python, C++, Steel Bank common lisp, and emacs lisp (these last two are probably not so common in industry).

In conjunction with my interest in learning programming languages, I find programming to be an extremely satisfying way to solve problems. Sometimes in research mathematics, you can get stuck to the point where you hardly know what to write down. In most programming projects, the building blocks of the solution are small enough to where you never really stop adding, improving, and fine-tuning. I, like many mathematicians, am stricken by a desire to solve puzzles, and programming scratches the itch well enough with a lower startup cost than mathematics.
